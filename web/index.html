The Flags

Three pattern checks (F01, F02, F03) run uniformly against every $1M+ federal prime contract in the dataset. The fourth card below describes the structural filter that strips false positives before flags publish. Together they are the entire screening framework. A fired flag is descriptive, not a finding: many flagged awards have routine explanations and are best read as worth a closer look.

▸ F01

No prior federal contracts plus sole-source above $10M

[expand]

What it checks. The award obligation exceeds ten million dollars, the recipient has no federal awards predating this one in the dataset, and the award was non-competitive.

Why it matters. A vendor with no prior federal history receiving a large non-competitive award is statistically uncommon. Both conditions independently warrant scrutiny; their co-occurrence at this dollar level is rare and worth a closer look.

▸ F02

No prior federal contracts plus competitive solicitation with one offer above $25M

[expand]

What it checks. The award obligation exceeds twenty-five million dollars, the recipient has no federal awards predating this one in the dataset, and the award was issued as a competitive solicitation that received only one offer.

Why it matters. A competitive solicitation that draws a single offer from a vendor with no prior federal history is statistically uncommon at this dollar threshold. Distinct from F01: F01 catches awards where competition was waived; this flag catches awards where competition was nominal but produced no contest.

▸ F03

No prior federal contracts plus first contract above $25M

[expand]

What it checks. The award obligation exceeds twenty-five million dollars, and the recipient has no federal awards predating this one in the dataset. Unlike F01 and F02, F03 does not consider how the contract is classified for competition. It fires regardless of whether the award is recorded as sole-source or competitive.

Why it matters. F03 catches awards that public reporting describes as effectively no-bid even when the formal procurement record classifies them differently. F01 catches awards explicitly recorded as sole-source; F02 catches competitive solicitations that drew only one offer. F03 closes the gap by treating the dollar size itself as the signal: a first contract above twenty-five million dollars to an entity with no federal history is statistically uncommon regardless of how the procurement was coded.

▸ SF

Structural filter: Flag matches stripped before publishing

[expand]

What it does. The structural filter is a second pass that removes flag matches with a known, non-anomalous explanation. What publishes is the subset that does not have one.

Why it matters. The flags rely on "no prior federal contracts" as a meaningful signal. That signal breaks when the entity is legally new but operationally familiar. A subsidiary spun up for one contract carries the parent's federal history under a different UEI. A joint venture formed for a single procurement is brand-new by definition. A facility operator with a fresh legal name continues work that has been federally funded for years. The filter removes these so the published set reflects entities actually new to federal business.
